# AHRQ R13 Grant — Project Summary / Abstract

Project Summary / Abstract from the AHRQ R13 conference grant "Artificial Intelligence and Emergency Medicine: Technology, Training, Self, and Society" (PIs: R. Andrew Taylor, MD, MHS — UVA; Jeremiah Hinson, MD — Johns Hopkins).

---

Artificial intelligence (AI) is poised to transform emergency medicine through enhanced clinical decision-making, improved operational efficiency, and personalized patient care. AI-driven technologies, including predictive diagnostic tools, real-time clinical decision support, and operational optimization systems, have rapidly evolved to address critical challenges faced by emergency departments (EDs). These emerging capabilities promise safer, more accurate, and efficient acute care delivery. However, future widespread integration of AI into emergency practice also raises important concerns regarding clinician readiness, patient safety, ethical considerations, and potential unintended consequences such as algorithmic bias and privacy violations. Despite the growing presence of AI in emergency care, many emergency medicine providers remain unfamiliar with core AI concepts, applications, and implications for clinical practice. Significant research gaps persist regarding effective strategies to integrate AI into clinical workflows in ways that complement rather than disrupt care delivery, preserve patient autonomy, and reduce risks of harm. Research is urgently needed to ensure AI implementation in emergency medicine is both effective and ethically sound.

In the 2026 Society for Academic Emergency Medicine (SAEM) Consensus Conference, titled "Artificial Intelligence and Emergency Medicine: Technology, Training, Self, and Society", we will convene experts and thought leaders from clinical practice, academia, industry, policy, ethics, and patient advocacy to critically examine the drivers of AI-driven emergency medicine, identify implementation challenges, and create a rigorous, actionable 10-year research agenda. SAEM consensus conferences are annual, structured, scientifically rigorous meetings with over two decades of success in crafting impactful research agendas for pivotal topics in emergency medicine. Through structured consensus methods, the proposed conference on Research Development will meet the following specific aims: (1) Develop a shared multi-stakeholder vision and conceptual framework for AI-driven emergency medicine, (2) Establish an actionable research agenda for the next decade addressing clinical decision support, operations, clinician-AI interactions, and ethical and societal implications, and (3) Identify critical educational and competency gaps for emergency providers in the era of AI. This consensus-driven agenda will guide future funded research, inform targeted educational initiatives, and foster cross-disciplinary collaboration, ultimately advancing emergency medicine practice toward safer, higher-quality, patient-centered care enhanced by responsible and robust AI technologies.
