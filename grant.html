AHRQ R13 · 2026 SAEM Consensus Conference

Artificial Intelligence and Emergency Medicine: Technology, Training, Self, and Society

The 2026 SAEM Consensus Conference is funded by an AHRQ R13 conference grant awarded to Andrew Taylor (UVA) and Jeremiah Hinson (Johns Hopkins). Its purpose is to convene experts across clinical practice, academia, industry, policy, ethics, and patient advocacy to define a national research and educational agenda on AI in emergency medicine. WG3 owns Aim 3 of the grant.

Why this matters for WG3: Our charge isn't speculative. The grant requires a structured educational needs assessment producing identified competency gaps and an educational roadmap. Our consensus output feeds directly into an AEM Education & Training manuscript and shapes how the next decade of EM training programs incorporate AI literacy.

The problem statement (from the grant)

AI is poised to transform emergency medicine through enhanced clinical decision-making, improved operational efficiency, and personalized patient care. Predictive diagnostic tools, real-time clinical decision support, and operational optimization systems have evolved rapidly to address critical ED challenges.

But integration also raises concerns about clinician readiness, patient safety, ethical considerations, algorithmic bias, and privacy. Despite the growing presence of AI in emergency care, many emergency physicians remain unfamiliar with core AI concepts, applications, and clinical implications. Significant research gaps persist regarding how to integrate AI in ways that complement rather than disrupt care delivery.

Source: AHRQ R13 Project Summary

The three aims

Aim 1

Shared vision & framework

Develop a shared multi-stakeholder vision and conceptual framework for AI-driven emergency care. Through evidence-informed keynotes, panels, and structured consensus-building (NGT), establish a common language and goals for integrating AI into clinical practice, operations, patient experience, and care delivery.

All WGs

Aim 2

10-year research agenda

Establish a rigorous, actionable 10-year research agenda using a modified Delphi process. Expert panels will identify and prioritize key research questions across clinical decision support, operations, infrastructure, training, human-AI interactions, and ethics. Agenda will specify priority areas, timelines, funding pathways, and patient-centered outcomes.

WGs 1, 2, 4, 5

Aim 3 · WG3

Educational & competency gaps

Identify critical educational and competency gaps in AI for emergency care providers. Through structured content analysis of conference outputs and participant interactions, conduct a comprehensive educational needs assessment. Pinpoint foundational knowledge gaps and emerging competencies EM professionals must acquire to integrate AI effectively and ethically.

How WG3's approach maps to Aim 3

The grant asks for a "structured content analysis of conference outputs and participant interactions" to surface foundational knowledge gaps and emerging competencies. WG3's scenarios-first approach is a deliberate choice about how to do that:

  1. Ground in real EM clinical work. Carl's 14 scenarios are concrete vignettes where AI is already deployed in the ED. They give the analysis empirical anchors instead of starting from a list of abstract competencies.
  2. Map against existing prior work. Makutonin's 2025 nominal-group themes give us a defensible baseline that's already been through one round of EM expert review.
  3. Surface gaps systematically. Where scenarios don't map cleanly onto existing themes, we have evidence — not opinion — for proposing new competency domains.
  4. Position alongside the published landscape. Triola & Rodman (2025), AAMC, Lee et al. (2024), AMEE Guide 178, and the AI-PACE Framework give us comparators so the WG3 output adds to — rather than duplicates — the existing literature.

The output: a competency framework, an assessment sketch, and a manuscript that satisfies Aim 3 and contributes to the field.
