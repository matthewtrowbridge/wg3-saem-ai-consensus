AimsSpecific Aims — the three R13 aims3 aims · ~470 words

Aim 1 · Shared vision & framework. Develop a shared multi-stakeholder vision and conceptual framework for AI-driven emergency care. Through evidence-informed keynotes, moderated interdisciplinary panels, and interactive breakout sessions employing structured consensus-building techniques (e.g., Nominal Group Technique), participants will collaboratively explore the transformative potential of AI in emergency medicine. The conference's consensus-driven dialogue will yield a unified vision and foundational understanding of "AI in Emergency Medicine," ensuring all stakeholders share a common language and goals for future innovation.

Aim 2 · 10-year research agenda. Establish a rigorous, actionable 10-year research agenda for AI in emergency medicine. Using a modified Delphi process, expert panels representing core subtopics — clinical decision support, operational management, AI infrastructure, training and education, human-AI interactions, and ethical and societal implications — will identify and prioritize key research questions. Conference participants will then refine and ratify these priorities through structured group discussions and plenary consensus voting, producing a finalized research roadmap with high-priority areas, timeline milestones, funding pathways, and patient-centered outcomes.

Aim 3 · Educational & competency gaps. WG3 owns this aim. Identify critical educational and competency gaps in AI for emergency care providers. Employing a structured content analysis of conference outputs and participant interactions, we will conduct a comprehensive educational needs assessment. The analysis will pinpoint foundational knowledge gaps and emerging competencies EM professionals must acquire to integrate AI effectively and ethically, guiding future curricula development and continuing professional education in AI literacy, human-computer interaction, and data-informed clinical decision-making.

Impact statement from the grant: Anticipated outcomes include publication of foundational white papers, delineation of critical knowledge gaps for future investigation, and development of consensus-informed strategies to promote the ethical, equitable, and effective integration of AI in emergency medicine.

SummaryProject Summary / Abstract~350 words

Artificial intelligence (AI) is poised to transform emergency medicine through enhanced clinical decision-making, improved operational efficiency, and personalized patient care. AI-driven technologies — predictive diagnostic tools, real-time clinical decision support, and operational optimization systems — have rapidly evolved to address critical challenges faced by EDs. These capabilities promise safer, more accurate, and efficient acute care delivery. However, widespread integration also raises concerns about clinician readiness, patient safety, ethical considerations, and unintended consequences such as algorithmic bias and privacy violations. Despite AI's growing presence in emergency care, many EM providers remain unfamiliar with core AI concepts, applications, and clinical implications. Significant research gaps persist regarding how to integrate AI into clinical workflows in ways that complement rather than disrupt care delivery, preserve patient autonomy, and reduce risks of harm.

The 2026 SAEM Consensus Conference — "Artificial Intelligence and Emergency Medicine: Technology, Training, Self, and Society" — will convene experts from clinical practice, academia, industry, policy, ethics, and patient advocacy to critically examine the drivers of AI-driven emergency medicine, identify implementation challenges, and create an actionable 10-year research agenda. Through structured consensus methods, the conference will (1) develop a shared vision and conceptual framework for AI-driven emergency medicine, (2) establish an actionable research agenda for the next decade, and (3) identify critical educational and competency gaps in the era of AI. The consensus-driven agenda will guide future funded research, inform targeted educational initiatives, and foster cross-disciplinary collaboration.

PlanConference Plan — structure & key factssections A–E

Andrew Taylor's original R13 Conference Plan covers five sections:

  • A. Significance — topic rationale, scientific need, meeting justification, AHRQ priority populations, stakeholder importance
  • B. Innovation — novel conference topic, consensus-building methods, strategic cross-sector partnerships
  • C. Approach — overview, objectives, attendees (~175), format/agenda, 3-phase consensus process (pre-conference Delphi · conference day · post-conference), dissemination, leadership committees, recruitment, evaluation, potential problems
  • D. Environment — Atlanta Marriott Marquis, SAEM infrastructure (8,200 members, ~40% attend annual meeting), funding structure, marketing
  • E. Impact — alignment with AHRQ priorities

Key facts from the grant:

  • Funding: AHRQ R13 conference grant
  • Co-chairs: Andrew Taylor (UVA) · Jeremiah Hinson (Johns Hopkins)
  • Co-sponsors: UVA · Johns Hopkins · SAEM
  • Original WG structure: 6 working groups in the grant (later consolidated to 5). WGs 1+2 merged into "AI in Clinical Practice and Operations"; original "Training, Competency, and Educational Innovation" became WG3.
  • Attendees: ~175 expected (half from general SAEM membership)
  • Keynote named in grant: Graham Walker, MD (Kaiser Permanente; founder, MDCalc)
  • Publications committed: AEM proceedings manuscript (10,000 words) + AEM E&T educational gaps manuscript (WG3's deliverable)
  • Track record: past SAEM consensus conferences generated a median of 22 grants and $20.5M in downstream funding each
  • Registration fee: ~$150/person
